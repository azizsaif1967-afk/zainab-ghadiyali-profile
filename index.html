The Secrets to Designing a Curiosity-Driven Career
Featured article on career philosophy, transferable skills, and beating impostor syndrome
▼

Zainab Ghadiyali's path to tech has been anything but conventional. Her resume spans engineering at Facebook, Product Lead at Airbnb, and co-founder of Wogrammer -- but the real story is in the extraordinary transitions that got her there.

At Every Crossroads, Lean Into Curiosity

Growing up middle-class in Mumbai with parents who never graduated college, Ghadiyali discovered her guiding principle in the stacks of local libraries. Books gave her the desire to learn about worlds different from her own -- and that curiosity became her career compass.

Creative vs. Reactive Mindset

Drawing from Bob Anderson's Leadership Circle framework, Ghadiyali distinguishes between two ways of thinking about uncertainty. The reactive mindset is fear-driven -- you see a problem as a threat, react to it, and spiral into anxiety about why you can't do something. The creative mindset is possibility-driven -- instead of fretting over problems, you emphasize what's possible. To shift from reactive to creative, she begins by setting an intention: reminding herself of the outcome she wants before entering difficult situations.

Career as a Painting, Not a Ladder

The traditional career ladder implies linear progression along a narrow track. Ghadiyali advocates thinking of your career as a work of art instead. From a distance you see a cohesive picture, but up close there are hundreds of separate brushstrokes. Each experience -- even ones that seem like detours -- adds a brushstroke. She studied alternative medicine in Berlin, considered becoming a doctor, graduated during the 2009 recession, got rejected from 200+ jobs, and worked for a public health nonprofit in Peru and Nicaragua before finding tech. On paper these seem like missteps, but with curiosity as the throughline, the connections emerge.

Three Decision-Making Questions

At every career juncture, Ghadiyali asks herself:

  1. Have I maximized growth in my current role? Don't move on prematurely. Soak up everything your current position offers.
  2. Does this align with my values? She creates a spreadsheet with options in rows and factors (growth, financial stability, location, manager quality, creativity) in columns, assigning point values.
  3. Do I want to expand laterally or dive deeper? Horizontal skills like communication and influence are essential even for deep specialists.

Reframe What It Means to Be "Qualified"

The key that unlocked Ghadiyali's door to tech was free Chinese food. During grad school, she stopped by an event advertising free food. It turned out to be a Facebook-sponsored hackathon. Before she could leave, a recruiter asked which team she was on. She sat at the one table with an empty chair. Her team won, earning an interview with Facebook.

The catch: it was an engineering interview, and she didn't code. She was tempted to postpone and learn first, but recalled a mentor's advice about jumping on the train when it's leaving the station. She accepted the interview, emphasizing her strengths as a logical thinker and quick learner rather than hiding her gaps. She minimized the hiring risk by noting it was only a three-month internship.

Leap Before You're Ready -- But Catch Up

Those first weeks at Facebook were brutal. She didn't know what Python was. She wanted to quit. Her manager told her to stop comparing herself to others with more experience. She spent the remaining weeks sleeping at the office, cramming code. By the end, she'd completed her project and earned a full-time offer as Production Engineer.

Her advice for landing in unfamiliar territory: be realistic about strengths and weaknesses, don't rest on laurels from previous roles, and stay open to feedback even when you disagree -- understanding the "why" behind it.

Tools for the Unconventional Path

The Power of Persuasion

At Airbnb, Ghadiyali needed to convince leadership to invest in re-architecting the technical stack -- a massive undertaking. She wasn't the VP, CTO, or CPO, but she learned to influence from a place without authority. She realized she'd been building this skill since convincing Facebook to hire an unqualified intern. Influence, she argues, is a life skill everyone exercises more than they realize -- from convincing parents about movies to negotiating seat upgrades.

Hire for Hunger, Not Checklists

As a hiring manager, Ghadiyali ignores requirements like "3-5 years experience." Instead, she defines the three most important qualities per role. The one universal quality she seeks: hunger -- not just motivation, but a genuine desire to learn. She asks candidates what they geek out about, whether they work on side projects, and when they last learned something completely new.

Builders vs. Smushers

Borrowing from Mary Lou Jepsen's wisdom: there are people who build sandcastles and people who smush them. Builders leave you feeling inspired after conversations. Smushers make you feel depleted and discouraged. Surrounding yourself with builders is the antidote to impostor syndrome -- they won't let you give in to fear. Unconventional careers make you more susceptible to impostor syndrome, making this even more critical.

Side Hustles as Skill Accelerators

In 2014, while at Facebook, Ghadiyali and colleague Erin Summers grew frustrated that women engineers were always asked about being women in tech rather than their technical accomplishments. They launched Wogrammer on Instagram, which grew into profiles, workshops, and journalism fellowships. After interviewing 200+ women engineers, they found that women across all levels -- from high school students to CEOs -- were most proud of relationships rather than professional achievements. Wogrammer set out to change that narrative.

Her advice for side projects: manage them like a business. Build scalable processes, bring in an executive director, create revenue models to minimize reliance on donations, and use automation to maximize limited time.

The Future is Curious

Ghadiyali encourages everyone in the workforce to remain adaptable. The jobs that will be most valuable require creative problem-solving, empathy, communication, and influence -- qualities that computers won't replicate for a long time. Whether through side projects, nonprofit work, or internal initiatives, developing soft skills is what will make you invaluable wherever you go.

How This AI Founder Fought Back Against Ransomware
Zainab's account of surviving a ransomware attack on Eat Cook Joy and the lessons for founders
▼

On a January morning, two weeks before Eat Cook Joy's public launch, Ghadiyali walked into a founder's nightmare. She was locked out of her own site. The platform she had built over two and a half years -- containing the most comprehensive data set for the private chef industry, worth millions of dollars -- had vanished.

FBI intelligence revealed the attack showed signs of a sophisticated international crime group targeting early-stage startups for ransom. Statistics show most business leaders in this position would negotiate and pay. Ghadiyali refused.

Clear Goals with the Right Team

Her goal was non-negotiable: recover all data, restore the platform, bring it online securely. She assembled a legal team led by Amy Mushahwar of Lowenstein Sandler -- people who understood the mission, anticipated the criminals' moves, and had strong law enforcement ties. One colleague, Tricia Wagner, spent her birthday consolidating evidence.

Fearless Strategic Execution

The strategy rested on three pillars: patience, detailed evidence, and fearlessness. When the crime ring denied having data or claimed it was deleted, Ghadiyali presented evidence that contradicted their claims. She knew the technical details of the platform better than they did. Meanwhile, she moved Eat Cook Joy operations offline to protect customer data, doubled engineering resources, and reinforced IP protections.

Within weeks of the ransom demand, the crime ring had transferred everything back.

Lessons for Founders

  • Set a clear goal. Define success upfront -- full recovery with no compromise.
  • Do not pay. Paying fuels more attacks and doesn't guarantee results.
  • Build the right team. Find lawyers with relevant experience, law enforcement relationships, and commitment to the mission.
  • Document everything. Evidence strengthens your position with law enforcement and adversaries.
  • Safeguard trust and capital. Protecting customers and investors must remain the top priority.

Two weeks after regaining control, Eat Cook Joy was back online. Within six months, their chef network tripled. The company is now on track for $1 million in annualized revenue, making it one of the fastest-growing startups in food tech.

Ghadiyali is now also building Stackbirds, a platform democratizing AI agent technology for the next generation of entrepreneurs.

Voyage San Antonio Magazine
Community Highlights: Meet Zainab Ghadiyali of Eat Cook Joy Inc
Full interview on founding Eat Cook Joy, challenges, and personal journey
▼
Where does your story begin?

Eat Cook Joy began with a simple idea: what if having a private chef could feel as natural and affordable as ordering takeout? Ghadiyali started it when she realized how many busy people loved home-cooked meals but didn't have time to plan, shop, cook, and clean. On the flip side, incredible chefs wanted to cook for people without the pressure of running a restaurant.

She first worked in Austin restaurant kitchens to understand what it's like to work as a chef. There she saw a massively talented chef pool looking to monetize skills outside the restaurant industry. She started small -- planning menus, shopping for groceries, and inviting chefs into people's kitchens to cook meals that felt both special and effortless, always including cleanup.

Today, Eat Cook Joy serves busy families, new parents, professionals, and anyone who wants to eat well without the stress. Their mission: make it easy to come home to good food and support the local chef economy.

Has it been a smooth road?

Not at all. Before starting Eat Cook Joy, Ghadiyali became a chef at restaurants and saw how talented chefs were stuck in rigid, exhausting environments. Many dreamed of taking skills directly to people but had no tools to make it work. Every profession has tools -- accountants have software, designers have platforms -- but chefs had nothing. That gap became Eat Cook Joy's foundation.

Personally, she didn't come from a food background. Learning to work with chef talent meant understanding an entirely new language -- from knife prep to sourcing seasonal ingredients. One turning point was partnering with Chef Chance Smith, who brought operational excellence and helped reimagine what's possible when great chefs get proper support.

What sets you apart?

Eat Cook Joy makes it easy for families to connect with local chefs at a fraction of the usual price. Weekly meal prep services offer personalized chef-prepared meals starting at $7 per serving. They leverage AI to help families customize meals per preferences, find grocery savings, and provide chefs with tools for successful execution. They now serve parties of all sizes in San Antonio, Austin, Dallas, and Houston.

How would we have described you growing up?

Ghadiyali's path has been anything but conventional. She moved to the US alone at 19 with $107, supported herself through college and graduate school. As Field Operations Manager for FIMRC, she built sustainable business models providing free pediatric care in some of the world's most remote regions. As a DAAD fellowship awardee, she conducted research at Europe's largest university hospital and published clinical trials.

As Tech Lead at Facebook, she led development of products used by over 1.5 billion people -- including Poke, advertising, Pages, and Blood Donations. She went on to serve as Product Lead at Airbnb and Canva. She co-founded Wogrammer, reaching 4+ million people and reshaping stories about women in engineering.

She holds a B.Sc. in Chemistry from Winthrop University and an M.Sc. in Industrial Engineering and Computer Science from the University of Wisconsin-Madison. For bridging the gender gap in technology, she was named one of Foreign Policy magazine's Top 100 Global Thinkers in 2015.

Meet the Wogrammers -- Women in Engineering
The origin story of Wogrammer and its ripple effect on women in tech
▼

Erin Summers and Zainab Ghadiyali were sure there was a better way. The two software engineers were en route to grab an afternoon snack after a long meeting when they got discussing how typical media coverage of women engineers was one-sided.

"As software engineers, we get asked a lot about what it's like to be a woman in tech. Are there any horror stories? Have we experienced sexism? We would much rather be asked about our technical accomplishments and the technology we've built."

The vision was clear: showcase women engineers -- from the newcomer learning to code to the CEO -- to show you can learn anywhere in the world and at any age.

The First Wogrammer

The very first person featured hadn't graduated high school yet. Summers' mentee from Girls Who Code, Morgan Lewis, had started the summer thinking code looked like someone "threw a keyboard at the wall." In just two weeks, she built a mobile app for location tracking and notification sending. With that profile, Wogrammer was born.

The Ripple Effect

In about a year, Summers and Ghadiyali featured 46 women programmers from across the tech industry. Posts focused on engineering accomplishments, how coding became part of their lives, what they're most proud of building, and advice for others. The best part: each feature created what they call "secondary connections" -- a ripple effect where featured women get invited to projects and events, inspiring others to get into coding.

Featured Wogrammers

  • Denae Ford -- CS Ph.D. candidate at NC State, published her first paper at CHASE 2015. Advised wogrammers to increase their network.
  • Leah Mcgowen-Hare -- Salesforce technical trainer. Received emails about daughters inspired to see someone like them in the field. Even got a request from Hour of Code in Suriname.
  • Ilona Bodnar -- Featured while still in high school. Self-taught coder who interned at Pixar. Became an instant role model with young girls asking technical questions.
  • Linda He -- CS student at Cal who learned iOS development. Her wogrammer advice brought her closer to friends who found it inspirational.
  • Kendell Byrd -- Self-taught coder with internships at Jawbone and JPMorgan. After being featured, was invited to speak at Urban Prep Academies.

Inside Outside Innovation - Episode 191
On Ignorance, Curiosity, and Persistence
Podcast featuring Zainab's journey from Airbnb to Wogrammer and the power of curiosity
▼

In this episode of Inside Outside Innovation, Ghadiyali shares the full arc of her career from arriving in the US with $107 to leading product at Airbnb, while co-founding a movement to reshape narratives about women in tech.

Key Topics Discussed

  • The power of "productive ignorance" -- Not knowing the rules can be an advantage. Ghadiyali didn't know she was "supposed to" have coding experience before interviewing at Facebook.
  • Curiosity as navigation -- Every career transition was driven by asking "what am I excited to learn next?" rather than following conventional paths.
  • Building Wogrammer -- How a side project born from frustration about media narratives grew into an organization reaching 4+ million people.
  • Persistence through rejection -- The 200+ job rejections that preceded her tech career, and why persistence isn't about stubbornness but about finding different angles.
  • Influence without authority -- Practical advice on how to drive change at organizations when you don't have the title or formal power.
